FYP: The Little Guardian

Can we design games that adapt to who the player is? For my FYP, I dived into the intersection of player traits and mechanical agency. The Little Guardian is a 3D side-scrolling platformer-adventure set in a decaying medieval world. It serves as the primary artifact for my undergraduate thesis, which investigates how game mechanics and player psychology intertwine to create deep engagement.

Part 1 : Development

Game Development and World Building

In this phase, I focused on creating a world that felt both atmospheric and mechanically responsive.

Game Trailer: The Little Guardian | 2.5D Side-Scrolling Platformer-Adventure Game

Game Project Overview

The Little Guardian is a 3D side-scrolling platformer-adventure set in a medieval, mysterious world. Players inhabit the role of a young guardian tasked with uncovering the source of a dark curse draining the life from a sacred forest. By blending platforming mechanics with RPG elements, the game invites players to explore a semi-open world filled with secrets, environmental puzzles, and combat challenges. To save the kingdom, players must master fluid movement and strategic problem-solving to confront the decay at the heart of the forest.

Development Process

As the sole creator of this project, I managed the full production lifecycle—balancing technical constraints with creative vision to deliver a polished 3D experience.

Ideation & Proposal

I initiated the project by defining the core vision: a synergy between dark fantasy aesthetics and player agency. This phase involved extensive brainstorming and market analysis to craft a solid project proposal that balanced creative storytelling with technical feasibility.

Player Preference Survey

Before development, I conducted a targeted survey to understand player mechanics preferences. This data allowed me to identify which interaction types fostered the strongest sense of agency, directly informing my game planning and core mechanic design.

World Building & Narrative

I meticulously designed a decaying medieval world where every asset, from the weathered stone bridges to the bioluminescent flora, serves a narrative purpose. By using layered fog and dynamic lighting, I created an atmosphere that visually reflects the encroaching curse.

Level Design & Player Flow

I prototyped levels using a "Graybox" method to test player pacing and navigation before committing to final art. This iterative process allowed me to refine puzzles and platforming challenges based on early playtest feedback.

Gameplay Programming & Mechanics

Using C# and Unity, I developed a responsive 3D side-scrolling movement system. I focused on the "feel" of the character—weight, jump arcs, and combat impact—to ensure that the Actual Mechanical Agency I was researching was felt directly through the controller.

Testing and Execution

I conducted structured playtests with 39 participants to validate my research and gameplay. By analyzing behavioral data and player feedback, I iteratively balanced the difficulty curves and refined the "Actual Mechanical Agency" to ensure a cohesive and engaging user journey.

Part 2 : Thesis Research

"To Investigate the Relationship Between Actual Mechanical Agency, Player Traits, and Engagement in Platformer-Adventure Games"

This research explores the psychological pillars of play, moving beyond aesthetics to understand the "Why" behind player immersion and long-term engagement.

Overview

This study explores how player traits influence perceived Actual Mechanical Agency (AMA) and engagement in Platformer-Adventure games. By focusing on AestheticGoal (AG) and Aesthetic-Challenge (AC) players, a game prototype featuring the top five preferred mechanics (identified via survey of 98 participants) was developed in Unreal Engine 5.3. A playtest with 39 players assessed perceived AMA and engagement through validated questionnaires. The results show that higher AMA significantly increases engagement, with AG players rating some mechanics slightly higher than AC players. The findings highlight the importance of trait-specific design for enhancing player engagement in Platformer-Adventure game.

Problem Statement

Despite the popularity of the Platformer-Adventure genre, standard design practices often overlook the critical intersection of precise gameplay metrics and diverse player psychology. This study addresses a key gap where user research, quantitative analysis, and player-centric mechanics are traditionally under-explored.

Limited Genre Studies

There is a significant scarcity of focused academic research specifically targeting the Platformer-Adventure genre.

AMA Under-explored

The concept of Actual Mechanical Agency (AMA) remains a theoretical gray area with little practical exploration.

Psychology Overlooked

Traditional design often ignores how individual player traits and preferences fundamentally alter the gaming experience.

Data Scarcity

A persistent lack of quantitative study and behavioral analysis to back up qualitative design assumptions.

Research Methodology

A sequential mixed-methods approach structured into three distinct phases, ensuring that the game mechanics were derived from player data and validated through empirical testing.

Phase 01

Preliminary Study & Identification

Conducted an initial survey with 100 participants to identify the Top 5 preferred game mechanics for the Platformer-Adventure genre.

  • Screened and categorized participants into AC (Aesthetic-Challenge) and AG (Aesthetic-Goal) player traits.
  • Mapped user preferences to core gameplay loops for the upcoming prototype.
Phase 02

Development & Implementation

Translated research insights into a functional 3D environment. This phase focused on Level Design and Mechanical Integration.

  • Implemented the identified "Top 5" mechanics into the The Little Guardian prototype.
  • Iterative graybox testing to ensure mechanics aligned with player expectations.
Phase 03

Evaluation & Quantitative Analysis

Conducted controlled playtests with 39 targeted players (19 AG / 20 AC) to evaluate the final artifact.

  • GSoA & GEQ Surveys: Measured psychological immersion and perceived game experience.
  • AMA Likert Scale: Quantified the relationship between Actual Mechanical Agency and engagement levels.
